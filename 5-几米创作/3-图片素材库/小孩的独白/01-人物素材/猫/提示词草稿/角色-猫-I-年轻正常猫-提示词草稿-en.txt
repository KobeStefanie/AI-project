A young blue-gray cat — around one year old, not a kitten anymore but not yet fully grown into an adult. Body neither fat nor thin — normal cat build, no extra weight, no muscle definition, just an ordinary-sized cat. From the side the body is a gentle oval, belly neither sagging nor tucked — a natural mild curve. Head proportion slightly larger relative to the body than an adult cat — about one-third of the body, that not-quite-grown-into-yet feeling of a young cat.

Fur: normal short-haired, neither fluffy nor tight-fitting. Blue-gray — the kind that hasn't been bleached by sun and rain, bluer and fresher than the faded gray of an old cat. Darkest along the spine, gradually lightening to pale gray-white toward the belly, smooth transition. Tail proportionally slightly longer than an adult cat's — young cats' tails always seem to grow faster than their bodies.

Eyes: slightly larger and rounder than an adult cat's — not vacant, just hasn't yet learned to narrow them into "I can't be bothered." Irises gray-green, a bit more translucent than the adult versions, pupils medium-sized. The gaze is not spacing out and not waiting — it's looking. Looking at a world it hasn't quite figured out yet. A little curious, but not much.

Ears: proportionally slightly larger than an adult cat's, the pale pink inside the ear a fresher pink — young cat skin hasn't thickened yet.

Legs: medium length, four clean white socks — the sock edges are soft watercolor washes, not crisp lines. A young cat's paw pads should still be soft.

Overall: this is a cat that hasn't seen much. It hasn't yet learned to half-close its eyes, hasn't learned that "moving is too much trouble," hasn't learned to keep its distance when a child cries. Its youth isn't about being energetic — it's about not yet having been worn smooth by time. Youth in a Jimmy Liao book — not innocence, just not yet.
