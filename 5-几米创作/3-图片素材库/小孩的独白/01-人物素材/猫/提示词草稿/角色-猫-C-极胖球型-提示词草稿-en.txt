Hand-painted watercolor illustration, Jimmy Liao picture book style. Loose ink line outlines — lines with slight tremble, broken strokes, ink density variation. Colored pencil cross-hatching layered over wet-on-wet watercolor washes, watercolor bleeding slightly beyond outlines, paper grain visible, handcrafted brushwork. Plain light cream background with faint watercolor wash and water-edge marks. Character design sheet, three views — front, side, back — arranged side by side.

An adult blue-gray fat cat — this one is genuinely very fat. From the front an irregular circle, the side contours bulging outward. From the side the belly sags dramatically — the lowest point nearly brushing the ground when walking. The mid-section of the side profile is pure arc — no waist, no taper, just a full curve from head to rump. Head round as if drawn with a compass — but because the body is so fat, the head proportionally reads as small. Ears on the small side — not actually small, but pushed inward by the cheek fat, appearing small. Ear tips rounder than other versions.

Eyes: round — unlike other versions, this cat's eyes are rounder. But the iris color is very pale, gray-green fading toward gray-white. Round eyes with no spark — not bright or alert, just open but not looking. Like two pale glass marbles. Eye spacing normal but on the fat face they appear pushed slightly apart by flesh.

Fur: short-haired but stretched tight over the bulk, making the fur look very short and close-fitting. Blue-gray leaning blue — a bit bluer than the standard version, like the color of an overcast evening sky. Belly and chest fur distinctly paler — pale to near cream-gray. Tail thick at the base — as fat as the body — then suddenly tapering at the tip, like the finishing stroke of a calligraphy brush.

Legs: short, mostly hidden by the belly. From the front almost no gap between the front legs — flesh crowds them together. Paw tips white.

Overall: the least agile version. It's not that it doesn't care — it's that moving is too much trouble. But that's exactly why, when this "too much trouble to move" cat rubs against your ankle, you feel it — it really meant to.
